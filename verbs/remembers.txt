BUSINESS/FINANCIAL NEWS (``f'' category)  TECH-BLACKBERRY (San Antonio) _ The BlackBerry has become one of the hottest technology devices for professionals. Since its introduction three years ago, the handheld device known for easily connecting executives to their e-mail has become an essential tool for mobile professionals. Today, more than 14,000 organizations use the BlackBerry and the number of BlackBerry subscribers exceeds 321,000, according to Research In Motion Ltd. or RIM, the Canadian company that manufacturers of the device. By L.A. LOREK.  SBC-FUTURE (San Antonio) _ SBC Communications Inc. is considered one of the bright stars in the beleaguered telecom section. It has successfully moved into data and long-distance services, outlasting its dot-com and start-up telecom critics. Now, SBC is trying to convince regulators to ease restrictions on its high-speed data services such as DSL (digital subscriber line) so it can better compete with the cable companies that are emerging as its ablest rivals. By SANFORD NOWLIN.  AUTO-ELDORADO-DEMISE (San Antonio) _ Just as Cadillac is poised to celebrate its 100th anniversary and the 50th anniversary of the introduction of the Eldorado Touring Coupe next year, the General Motors luxury division is dropping the Eldorado line. After the 2002 model year, there will be no more Eldorado. By G. CHAMBERS WILLIAMS III.  LIFESTYLE/FEATURES (``l'' category)  PROM-DRESSES (Albany, N.Y.) _ As if high school is not filled with enough angst, it's topped off by the prom. But when it comes to prom dresses, girls don't need to spend all they have earned baby-sitting or folding shirts at The Gap. A funky and frugal alternative to high-priced gowns can be found at secondhand or consignment stores. By KRISTI L. GUSTAFSON.  BIRTHDAY-107 (San Antonio) _ William Martin Sr. remembers the day his ``dear mama'' died as if it were yesterday, even though she's been gone for almost 100 years. His father, a slave's son who built railroads, had boarded a train bound  to Missouri early that day. His father eventually returned, and later married a woman who treated Martin very well, he remembers, but he still misses his mama. ``I'm 107 years old,'' said the former railroad worker and farmer. ``I've been here a long time. I never would have thought I would have been a living man today.'' By MACARENA HERNANDEZ.  TRAVEL (``t'' category)  TRAV-EVERGLADES (Flamingo, Fla.) _ The Florida Everglades will never win the prize for best national park. It hasn't the mystique of Mesa Verde, the vistas of Acadia or the awesome wonder of the Grand Canyon. And what with the heat, the humidity and the mosquitoes, the Everglades never will be confused with Miss Congeniality. Yet the Everglades is full of marvels: purple sunsets, skies obliterated by great flocks of ibis, ruler-length alligators clinging to their mother's back, night-blooming orchids and glistening spider webs threading through a sawgrass-lined slough. By SYD KEARNEY.  With: TRAV-EVERGLADES-INFO  TRAV-PLANES-COLUMN (Undated) _ In this post-9/11 world of intense security and uncertainty, many of us continue to agonize about the feasibility of an occasional long-distance vacation or business trip. Yet several times each week, tens of thousands of flight attendants go to an airport, endure the security screening process and board a plane. Just to go to work. By HARRY SHATTUCK.  TRAV-ST.AUGUSTINE (St. Augustine, Fla.) _ As dusk creeps in on this old city, small groups of people begin clustering. In numbers of 20 to 30, they gather around leaders in strangely ancient-looking garb, clothes reminiscent of, say, a  Colonial-era midwife or minister, or a turn-of-the-century gravedigger. Usually, the guides carry a candle or lantern to keep the crowd together. These dusky gatherings are ``ghost tours,'' the latest manifestation of the tourist trade in this city that owes its prosperity to history. BY JAMES R. GRAY.  SPORTS (``s'' category)  PAN-AM-GAMES (Rio De Janeiro) _ The head of the Brazilian Olympic Committee has asked San Antonio to give up its Pan American Games bid and allow Rio to be the host of the competition in 2007. In an impassioned appeal to the organizers in Texas, Carlos Nuzman said the United States should give other countries a chance to organize and run international sporting events. By ANDREW DOWNIE and DITI KINKHABWALA.  COMMENTARY (``k'' category)  U.S.-VENEZUELA (Washington) _ The conventional wisdom that ``There are no second acts in life,'' got turned on its head earlier this month when Venezuelan President Hugo Chavez was removed from office by a citizen uprising, then returned to power 48 hours later when the nation's interim government proved less democratic than the one it had replaced. In the process, I and many other Latin America observers were  caught flatfooted, at once hopeful for a new beginning in Venezuela and uneasy over the way it came about. By THOMAS F. McLARTY III  MIDEAST-POWELL (Washington) _ When Secretary of State Colin Powell departed for the Middle East, U.S. policy seemed to have turned a corner. In response to the gruesome violence and mayhem in the West Bank and Israel, and to prevent the unraveling of our own war on terrorism, President Bush rightly engaged the prestige of his office for the first time to pull both sides away from the abyss that threatened to engulf the entire region. By EDWARD S.
